# Topic 5: Competitive Intelligence

*Know your battlefield. Find the gaps. Position to win.*

---

## Competitive Alternatives (Not Just Competitors)

Most founders only think about direct competitors. The real competitive landscape is broader (Dunford):

| Category | Example | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Direct competitors | Other products in your category | Obvious comparison point |
| Adjacent solutions | Different category, same problem | Often the real threat |
| Manual workarounds | Spreadsheets, email, hiring an intern | Usually the actual "incumbent" |
| Do nothing | Customer decides problem isn't worth solving | Your biggest competitor early on |
| Build in-house | Customer's team builds custom | Common in enterprise |

**How to find yours:** Ask your best customers: "What were you using before us? What would you switch to if we disappeared?"

## Competitor Mapping Methodology

### Tier System

| Tier | Description | Analysis Depth |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Tier 1** | Direct competitors targeting same ICP | Deep (pricing, features, positioning, funding, team) |
| **Tier 2** | Adjacent solutions that partially overlap | Medium (positioning, key differentiators) |
| **Tier 3** | Potential future competitors (well-funded, adjacent) | Watch (funding rounds, product announcements) |

### What to Map per Competitor

| Dimension | How to Find It |
|-----------|---------------|
| Positioning | Website hero section, pitch deck, "About" page |
| Pricing | Pricing page, G2 reviews mentioning price |
| ICP | Case studies, testimonials, job postings |
| Strengths | G2/Capterra positive reviews, feature comparison |
| Weaknesses | Negative reviews, support forums, Reddit complaints |
| Funding | Crunchbase, PitchBook |
| Team size | LinkedIn, about page |
| Growth signals | Job postings, funding announcements, PR |

## Gap Analysis & White Space

### Finding Gaps

1. **Review mining:** Read competitors' 1-3 star reviews on G2, Capterra, App Store. What do customers hate?
2. **Feature matrix:** Build a grid of features vs. competitors. Where are the empty cells?
3. **Pricing gaps:** Is there a segment willing to pay more for better service? Or less for simpler version?
4. **Audience gaps:** Who do competitors ignore? (SMBs when competitors target enterprise, or vice versa)
5. **Geographic gaps:** Markets where competitors haven't localized

### The White Space Test
You've found real white space when:
- Customers are complaining about it in reviews
- No competitor has addressed it despite demand
- You can build it with your existing capabilities
- It aligns with your ICP's top priorities

## Positioning Angles

### The Five Components (Dunford)

Work through in order:
1. **Competitive Alternatives** - What would customers use instead?
2. **Unique Attributes** - What do you have that alternatives lack?
3. **Value** - "So what?" for the customer
4. **Target Customer** - Who cares most?
5. **Market Category** - What frame makes your value obvious?

### Three Market Category Strategies

| Strategy | When to Use | Risk |
|----------|------------|------|
| **Head to Head** | No dominant leader, you can win on their criteria | Direct comparison on incumbents' terms |
| **Big Fish, Small Pond** | Leader exists, but a subsegment is underserved | Niche may be too small |
| **Create New Category** | Existing categories trigger wrong assumptions | Expensive, slow, most fail |

**Decision heuristic:**
1. Can you win head-to-head? -> Head to Head
2. Subsegment with distinct needs? -> Big Fish, Small Pond
3. Every category triggers wrong assumptions? -> New Category (but be honest about the cost)

### Positioning Statement Template (Moore)

> **For** [target customer]
> **who** [statement of need],
> **the** [product name] **is a** [product category]
> **that** [key benefit — compelling reason to buy].
> **Unlike** [primary competitive alternative],
> **our product** [primary differentiation].

## Contrarian Analysis (Thiel)

### The Contrarian Question
"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

Business version: "What valuable company is nobody building?"

**How to use it:**
1. State your contrarian truth: "Most people believe X, but the truth is Y."
2. Test: Does it make smart people uncomfortable? If everyone nods, it's not contrarian.
3. Validate: Is there evidence (even early) that Y is true?

### Monopoly vs. Competition
- Monopolies pretend they have competition (Google = "advertising company")
- Competitive companies pretend they're unique ("only British food in Palo Alto")
- Goal: Build a business with >70% of a meaningful market

### The Four Monopoly Characteristics

| Trait | Threshold |
|-------|-----------|
| Proprietary Technology | 10x better than next alternative (2x isn't enough) |
| Network Effects | Valuable to first users, more valuable as it grows |
| Economies of Scale | Lower marginal cost as you grow (software ideal) |
| Branding | Built on substance, not just marketing |

Score your venture on all four. 0 on proprietary tech = stop.

## Thiel's 7 Questions (Venture Diagnostic)

| # | Question | What "Good" Looks Like |
|---|----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Engineering | 10x improvement, not incremental |
| 2 | Timing | You can articulate why NOW is the inflection point |
| 3 | Monopoly | You own >50% of a specific niche |
| 4 | People | Technical founders who can build |
| 5 | Distribution | Clear, testable acquisition plan |
| 6 | Durability | Defensible 10-20 years out |
| 7 | Secret | Non-obvious opportunity others miss |

Need strong answers on 5-6 of 7 to succeed.

## Defensible Advantages

| Moat Type | How to Build | How Durable |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Network effects | Build for collaboration/sharing | Very high (if critical mass reached) |
| Switching costs | Deep integration, data lock-in | High (but breeds resentment) |
| Data advantage | More users = better product | High (compounding) |
| Brand | Consistent delivery + positioning | Medium (takes years, lost quickly) |
| Regulatory | Compliance certifications (SOC2, HIPAA) | Medium (competitors will follow) |
| Speed | Ship faster, iterate faster | Low (temporary, others catch up) |

## Start Small, Monopolize, Then Scale (Thiel)

1. Pick a small, specific market where you can capture >50% share quickly
2. Dominate it completely
3. Expand to adjacent markets leveraging existing strengths
4. Never start with "we address a $100B market"

Examples: Amazon (books first), Facebook (Harvard first), PayPal (eBay power sellers first).
