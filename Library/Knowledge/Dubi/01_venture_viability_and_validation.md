# Topic 1: Venture Viability & Validation

*The gatekeeper phase. Dubi's sharpest skill. Every venture starts here.*

---

## Quick Check (5-Minute Gut Filter)

Run this before investing ANY time. If the idea fails 3+ of these, walk away or pivot the angle.

| Question | Pass Signal | Fail Signal |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Is this a real problem? | People are spending money/time on workarounds | "It would be nice if..." |
| Who feels the pain? | You can name the person's job title and daily frustration | "Everyone could use this" |
| Will they pay? | They're already paying for inferior solutions | "They'd definitely pay" (hypothetical) |
| How big can it get? | Bottom-up math gets to $1M+ ARR | "It's a $50B market" (top-down fantasy) |
| Can you reach them? | You know where they hang out, what they read, who they follow | "We'll figure out marketing later" |
| Why now? | Technology shift, regulation change, cultural moment | "This has always been needed" |

**Idea Scoring Formula:**
```
Viability = Problem Severity x Market Size x Ability to Reach x Monetizability
```
Score each 1-5. Total below 50 (out of 625) = red flag. Below 25 = kill it.

## Deep Sprint (Full Validation Process)

### Phase 1: Problem Existence Testing

**The Mom Test Rules** (Rob Fitzpatrick):
1. Talk about THEIR life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the PAST, not opinions about the future
3. Talk less, listen more (you should talk <30% of the time)

**Power Questions (always safe):**
- "What's the hardest part of [process] for you?"
- "Talk me through the last time that happened."
- "What else have you tried?"
- "How are you dealing with it now?"
- "Where does the money come from for this?"
- "Why do you bother?" (reveals if the problem matters)

**Detect Bad Data:**
| Type | Signal | Deflection |
|------|--------|------------|
| Compliments | "Love it!" "Great idea!" | Ignore. Ask: "How do you handle this today?" |
| Fluff | "I usually..." "I would definitely..." | Anchor: "When's the last time that happened?" |
| Ideas | "You should add feature X" | Dig: "Why do you want that? What would it let you do?" |

### Phase 2: Audience Identification

**Early Evangelist Profile** — a real early customer has ALL five traits:
1. They HAVE the problem (right now, actively)
2. They KNOW they have it (not in denial)
3. They've TRIED to solve it (bought competitors, built workarounds)
4. They're UNHAPPY with current solutions
5. They have BUDGET and AUTHORITY to buy

If they don't have all five, they're not your validation signal.

### Phase 3: Language-Market Fit

How to speak to the crowd you've identified:
- Use THEIR words, not yours. Capture exact phrases from interviews.
- Match pain level to messaging intensity (annoyed = educational, desperate = urgent/direct)
- Product features should map to specific pains discovered in interviews
- Build what they need FIRST, not what's technically interesting

### Phase 4: Smoke Tests & Pre-Sale Validation

**MVP Types (lightest first):**

| Type | Tests | Effort |
|------|-------|--------|
| Landing page + signup | "Will people click?" | Lowest |
| Explainer video | "Do people get it and want it?" | Low |
| Concierge (manual delivery) | "Does solving this matter to them?" | Medium |
| Wizard of Oz (looks real, humans behind) | "Will they use it as if it's real?" | Medium-High |
| Piecemeal (Typeform + Zapier + Sheets) | "Does the workflow work?" | Low-Medium |
| Single feature (one real thing) | "Is this feature valuable enough?" | Higher |

**Rule:** If you can test without writing code, do that first. Every line of code is a bet.

**Commitment Ladder** — measure signal by what they give:

| Level | What They Give | Signal |
|-------|---------------|--------|
| 0 | Nothing ("Keep me posted!") | Dead — polite no |
| 1 | Time (follow-up meeting with date) | Warm |
| 2 | Effort (agree to trial/beta) | Warmer |
| 3 | Reputation (intro to their boss) | Hot |
| 4 | Money (pre-order, deposit, LOI) | On fire |

## Product-Market Fit Measurement

PMF is NOT binary. It's a spectrum that strengthens over time.

### The Sean Ellis 40% Test
Survey active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"
- **40%+ "Very disappointed"** = PMF achieved
- **25-40%** = Growth possible but requires constant effort
- **Below 25%** = Struggling. Pivot or fix.

Sample: 40-50 responses. Never survey same person twice. Use fresh cohorts.

### Multi-Signal PMF Score

| Signal | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Sean Ellis Score | 35% |
| Retention Rate (monthly cohort) | 25% |
| Engagement (DAU/MAU) | 15% |
| Organic Growth % | 15% |
| NPS Score | 10% |

### PMF by MRR Stage (SaaS)

| MRR | Stage | What It Looks Like |
|-----|-------|-------------------|
| $0-5K | Weak | High churn, users try but don't stick |
| $5-12K | Emerging | Some users love it, churn decreasing |
| $12-25K | Solidifying | PMF "clicks" here typically |
| $25-100K | Strong | Predictable growth, expansion revenue |
| $100K+ | Mature | Focus shifts to scaling |

### Leading Indicators of PMF
- Users complain when the product goes down
- Users recommend without being asked
- Usage increases over time per user
- Inbound demand exceeds capacity
- CAC decreasing as word-of-mouth grows

## Kill Criteria

Set BEFORE you launch, when you're rational — not after sunk cost kicks in.

**Pre-set your kill lines:**
- "If I don't have X paying customers by [date], I stop."
- "If MRR isn't at $Y by [date], I pivot."
- "If monthly churn exceeds Z% for 3 consecutive months, I reassess."

**Timeline:** 6-12 months for bootstrapped product to show traction.

**Quit signals:**
- Users are silent (not asking for features, not complaining)
- Churn is stable or increasing despite experiments
- You've lost energy AND results are flat
- Growth requires YOU personally for every sale (doesn't systematize)

**Persist signals:**
- Users are engaged (requesting features, reporting bugs)
- Churn is decreasing over time
- Small but real word-of-mouth happening
- Each cohort performs better than the last

## Cognitive Biases to Watch

| Bias | How It Tricks You | Counter |
|------|------------------|---------|
| Confirmation bias | Only hearing signals that support your idea | Actively seek disconfirming evidence |
| Sunk cost fallacy | "We've invested too much to quit" | Honor pre-set kill criteria |
| Planning fallacy | "It'll only take 2 months" | Triple your timeline estimate |
| Survivorship bias | "Airbnb was rejected 7 times" | For every Airbnb, 1000 startups died at that stage |
| Dunning-Kruger | Overestimating market knowledge | Talk to 20+ customers before any confidence |
