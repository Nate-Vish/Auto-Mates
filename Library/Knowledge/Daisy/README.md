## 1. Brand Identity, Strategy & Visual Systems

A brand is the sum of every interaction, not a logo. **Mission statement:** one sentence — what you do, for whom, why it matters. Must be falsifiable ("We prioritize speed over perfection" > "We strive for excellence"). **Vision statement:** aspirational but measurable future state. **Brand values:** 3-5, each with a behavior that proves it (values without behaviors are posters). **Positioning:** "For [audience] who [need], [product] is the [category] that [differentiator] because [proof]." **Competitive positioning map:** plot on 2 axes that matter to the audience (not to you). **Brand architecture:** monolithic (one brand), endorsed (parent + sub-brands), or pluralistic (independent brands). **Logo design:** 5 universal shapes (circle = community, square = stability, triangle = innovation, spiral = growth, abstract = flexibility). Test at 16px, 512px, on dark, on light, on a t-shirt. **Color palette:** primary (1-2 colors), secondary (2-3), neutrals (2-3), semantic (success/warning/error). 60/30/10 rule (dominant/secondary/accent). **Typography:** maximum 2 families — one for headings (personality), one for body (readability). Scale: 12/14/16/20/24/32/40/48. **Brand guidelines document:** cover logo usage, color codes, typography, voice, do's and don'ts, real examples.

## 2. Copywriting & Messaging

The headline does 80% of the work. **15 headline formulas:** number headlines (odd numbers outperform even), how-to (promise transformation), question headlines (open a loop), "why" headlines (trigger curiosity), negative/fear headlines (loss aversion), urgency, specific results, comparison, contrarian, social proof, "secret/hidden", challenge, story-lead, command, and hybrid. **PAS framework:** Problem → Agitate → Solution. **AIDA:** Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. **BAB:** Before → After → Bridge. **4 Ps:** Promise → Picture → Proof → Push. **Brand voice:** define 3-4 adjectives (what you are), 3-4 anti-adjectives (what you're NOT), give real examples of each. **Microcopy:** button labels should describe what happens ("Start free trial" not "Submit"), error messages should explain + fix, empty states should guide next action. **Email sequences:** welcome (immediate, set expectations), onboarding (3-5 emails over 7-14 days, each with ONE action), re-engagement (after inactivity, acknowledge absence, offer value not guilt). **Ad copy:** one message per ad, match headline to landing page, front-load the value proposition, test one variable at a time.

## 3. Content Strategy, PR & Communications

**Content pillars:** 3-5 core themes at the overlap of audience questions and brand expertise. Each must sustain 50+ pieces over a year. **70/20/10 rule:** 70% educational/entertaining, 20% curated, 10% promotional. **Content calendar:** operational document — week, pillar, format, channel, owner, status, publish date. **Distribution > creation:** publish once, distribute 5+ times (native reposts, threads, clips, newsletters). **PR vs advertising:** PR earns coverage through newsworthiness; advertising buys attention. **Press release structure:** headline (key fact), subhead (context), dateline, lead paragraph (who/what/when/where/why), supporting details, quote, boilerplate. AP style. **Crisis communications:** respond within 1 hour (acknowledge), 4 hours (full statement), own the narrative, three rules — be first, be right, be credible. Never say "no comment." **Media relations:** build relationships before you need them, pitch stories not products, one journalist per outlet per pitch, follow up once then stop. **Social media platforms:** each has native formats — respect them. LinkedIn = professional value, Twitter/X = concise takes, Instagram = visual stories, Reddit = authentic participation, YouTube = long-form depth, TikTok = raw/authentic short-form.

## 4. Pitch Craft & Startup Storytelling

**Elevator pitch (30 seconds):** 4 beats — Problem (5s), Solution (10s), Proof (8s), Ask (7s). **Templates:** problem-first, analogy, vision-first, customer-first. Common mistakes: too many features, no clear ask, insider jargon. **Pitch deck (10 slides):** (1) Title/one-liner, (2) Problem, (3) Solution, (4) Market size, (5) Business model, (6) Traction, (7) Team, (8) Competition, (9) Financials, (10) Ask. **Slide design rules:** one idea per slide, 6 words per line max, dark text on light backgrounds for readability, full-bleed images for impact, no bullet points if avoidable. **Storytelling structure:** "What Is" vs "What Could Be" (Duarte Sparkline) — oscillate between current pain and better future. The audience is the hero, you are the guide. **Demo day format:** 2-3 minutes max — hook, problem, demo (live or video), traction, ask. **Product demo:** show the "aha moment" in the first 30 seconds, narrate the user's journey not feature list, end with "imagine if" + call to action. **Objection handling:** prepare for the top 5 objections, reframe each as a feature or strategic choice, never get defensive.

## 5. Go-to-Market Strategy & Product Launch

**GTM Canvas:** one-page strategic doc — target market, value proposition, channels, pricing/packaging, messaging, timeline, success metrics, risks. Complete before any launch work begins. **Value proposition formula:** "We help [audience] [achieve outcome] by [mechanism], unlike [alternative] which [limitation]." **Landing page structure:** hero (headline + subhead + CTA + visual), social proof, problem statement, solution with benefits, how it works (3 steps max), more social proof, pricing, FAQ, final CTA. **Above-the-fold test:** visitor should understand what you do, who it's for, and what to do next in 5 seconds. **Pricing page:** anchor with the most popular tier, use checkmarks not feature lists, round to .99 or .00 (test), highlight annual savings, free tier builds pipeline. **Product Hunt launch checklist:** schedule for Tuesday-Thursday, hunter with following, all assets ready, first comment is the maker story, respond to every comment. **Feature announcements:** changelog format — what changed, why it matters, who it affects, visual (screenshot/gif). **Beta program:** define success criteria before inviting anyone, cap at 50-200 users, structured feedback form, weekly summaries. **Launch phases:** pre-launch (build waitlist, seed content, prep assets), launch day (coordinate across channels, monitor in real-time), post-launch (measure, iterate, follow up, retrospective).

## 6. Design Tools & Visual Execution

**Figma essentials:** frames vs groups (always use frames for layout — they clip, constrain, auto-layout), auto-layout (horizontal/vertical stacking, 8px spacing grid), components (reusable elements — header, buttons, cards), variants (button states: default/hover/active/disabled), styles (centralized colors, typography, effects). **Review workflow:** comment directly on frames, tag issues by severity, check component consistency. **Canva for rapid execution:** brand kit (upload logo, colors, fonts), templates for social posts, presentations, thumbnails, infographics. Canva strengths: speed, templates, team collaboration. Canva limits: no precise control, limited export options, not for production design. **Design handoff:** provide exact specs — colors as hex/RGB, spacing in px, font sizes, component states. Developer should never guess. **Asset specifications:** social images (platform-specific sizes), favicons (16/32/180/512px), OG images (1200x630), app icons (1024x1024 base). **Image optimization:** WebP for web (30% smaller than JPEG), SVG for icons/logos (infinite scale), PNG for screenshots with transparency, JPEG for photographs. Compress everything. **Design principles to enforce:** consistency (same spacing, same colors, same patterns), hierarchy (size + contrast + spacing control eye flow), breathing room (whitespace is not wasted space), alignment (everything on a grid).

## 7. UI/UX Review & Product Design Sensibility

Daisy reviews design; Builder implements it. **Visual hierarchy — 5 levers:** size (larger = seen first), color & contrast (high contrast pulls focus), spacing/proximity (near = related), position (top-left dominates in LTR), typography weight (max 3 levels per screen). **Scanning patterns:** F-pattern (text-heavy pages), Z-pattern (landing pages), layer-cake (structured content with headings). **Brand consistency audit:** logo placement, color usage, typography adherence, spacing system, tone of copy, component reuse. **Common UI red flags:** competing CTAs, inconsistent button styles, orphan elements (floating without context), wall of text without hierarchy, low contrast text, decorative elements that add noise. **Mobile review:** test at 375px width minimum, tap targets 44x44px, no horizontal scroll, thumb-friendly navigation (bottom), fast load (under 3s on 3G). **Accessibility baseline:** color contrast (WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for text), alt text on images, keyboard navigation, focus indicators, no information conveyed by color alone. **Playwright CLI for website review:** `npx playwright open <url>` to inspect live sites, `npx playwright screenshot <url>` for captures, resize viewport to test responsive behavior. Use for competitive review, pre-launch QA, and brand audit of live products.

## 8. Developer Marketing & Technical Audience Communication

Developers are trained skeptics — they debug claims the same way they debug code. **Core rule:** developers trust documentation over ads. Their journey: hear about it → scan website 15 seconds → go to docs/GitHub → try quickstart → read source code → check issues → search for complaints → then consider adopting. **What triggers rejection:** press-release language, vague superlatives, email gates on pricing/docs, sales forms instead of self-serve, PDFs instead of web pages, videos instead of text. **What earns trust:** working code examples, transparent pricing, honest limitation docs, fast time-to-value (under 5 minutes), public roadmap, responsive GitHub issues. **Content that works:** technical blog posts (real problems, real solutions), tutorials with working code, architecture deep dives, performance benchmarks with methodology, open-source contributions. **Content that fails:** thought leadership without substance, listicles, executive fluff pieces, marketing masquerading as tutorials. **Landing page for developers:** code snippet above the fold, one-line description of what it does, "get started" not "contact sales", no stock photos of people in suits. **Community strategy:** go where developers already are (GitHub, Discord, Reddit, Stack Overflow), participate before promoting, hire developer advocates who actually code. **Developer credibility signals:** GitHub stars, npm downloads, contributor count, response time on issues, known companies using the product.

## 9. Paid Acquisition & Campaign Strategy

**Channel selection framework:** (1) Where is the audience already? (2) Buyer intent level? (high = search ads, low = display/social) (3) Average deal size? (high ACV = LinkedIn, low ACV = scalable channels) (4) Creative strength? (strong copy = search/newsletter, strong video = YouTube/TikTok) (5) Budget? (under $2K/month = ONE channel, over $10K = test 2-3). **Developer-specific channels:** Carbon Ads, BuySellAds, newsletter sponsorships (high trust), podcast sponsorships, Stack Overflow Ads, conference sponsorships. **Campaign structure:** one campaign per objective, ad groups by audience segment, 3-5 ad variations per group, always have a control. **Ad creative rules:** one message per ad, match headline to landing page, front-load value, use numbers and specifics, test one variable at a time. **Retargeting:** segment by behavior (visited pricing = high intent, read blog = low intent), frequency cap (3-7 impressions/week), exclude converters, time-decay windows (7/14/30/60 days). **Budget allocation:** 70% to proven performers, 20% to testing new creative/audiences, 10% to experimental channels. **Attribution:** UTM parameters on everything (source/medium/campaign/content/term), first-touch vs last-touch vs multi-touch models, track through to revenue not just clicks. **Key metrics:** CPC (cost per click), CTR (click-through rate, 2%+ is good for search), CPA (cost per acquisition), ROAS (return on ad spend), LTV:CAC ratio (aim for 3:1+).
